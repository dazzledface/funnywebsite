Course description

COMP 126 is a ground-up introduction to the fundamentals of modern HTML, CSS, and user interface (UI) design. Students will design, build, and deploy static websites according to current best practices for responsive, usable, inclusive, and accessible web design.

Target audience

This introductory course is accessible to all students from all backgrounds. Aspiring web developers, entrepreneurs, visual artists, librarians, humanists (digital and non), programmers, performers, poets, hobbyists, scientists, educators, and others are all equally valued and welcome. No prior technical expertise or programming experience is assumed, required, or necessary.

Here's what this course covers: the fundamentals of modern HTML5 and CSS3; responsive web design; web accessibility standards; usability and user interface (UI) design; and a very gentle, very basic introduction to JavaScript, libraries, and a couple of front-end CSS/JS frameworks.

Here's what this course does not cover: much web programming proper (HTML and CSS are not technically "programming languages"); databases; dynamic web app development; APIs; full-stack web dev frameworks. Experienced programmers and developers please note: these tools and methods are crucial to modern web development in the field, but this is a fundamentals course--so although we'll discuss them, their use is beyond the scope of this class.


Goals & objectives

The primary goal of this course is to help students become competent advanced-beginner- to intermediate-level web designers, independent learners, and capable problem solvers through a project-based approach to responsive web and UI design and development and its associated troubleshooting strategies. To this end, our primary objectives and planned learning outcomes are as follows.

By the end of this course, students will:

  • Possess an understanding of the basic and necessary processes and skills involved in front-end responsive web design and development;
  • Possess a working HTML5 and CSS3 skillset;
  • Understand and be able to employ modern UI design principles;
  • Possess the knowledge necessary to effectively pursue further formal or self-motivated education in more advanced web development tools and skills;
  • Have developed a well-designed, well-functioning live web site suitable for inclusion in, or as, a professional, artistic, and/or academic portfolio.

Final project

In the COMP 126 final project, you'll form teams of 2-4 people and operate as a web design and development team hired to build a site for a client. I encourage you to find a “real” client for a “real” project among yourselves or in the community: an individual, group, club, company, whatever. The important thing is to have a way to define a client’s needs, goals, and priorities for the design and build of the site; why not offer your web development services to a group in need?


Quizzes, homework, & exams

Unless specified otherwise, assignments will be scored on a 1-10 scale according to a rubric that will be provided for each. Quizzes, exams, and the final project will be scored between 0 and 100. I generally do not need to apply curves to exam scores, and I do not drop a lowest quiz score. I also do not offer extra credit assignments: what you see is what you get.

Quizzes will usually comprise 10 multiple-choice questions and be delivered on Sakai with a 30-minute time limit, open book, open notes, open everything. (The professor reserves the right to alter this format; notice will be given of any changes.) Quiz make-ups will be granted when accompanied by a University Excused Absence or when arranged and approved by the professor at least 24 hours in advance. We do not drop a lowest quiz score.

The midterm exam will be half multiple-choice, half a coding exercise in which you'll be given an image of a website layout you must re-create using HTML and CSS according to a set of given parameters. Rescheduling or making up the midterm requires a University Excused Absence.

The final exam will be multiple-choice only, since your final project—a fully developed, live website—is due at the same time and covers the coding aspect. It will be administered in compliance with UNC's final exam regulations and scheduled according to the UNC Final Exam calendar. Rescheduling or making up a final exam requires an exam excuse letter from the University. No exceptions.


Late assignments, extensions, & resubmission

Regular assignments (that is, non-exam- or final-project-related) assignments will be accepted for credit only if submitted within two weeks after each assignment's original due date. Late submissions during this two-week window will be penalized about -0.143 of a point per day (or portion of a day), with a total max deduction of 2/10 points. After that two-week window has passed, late submissions for that assignment will no longer be accepted. Note that this only applies until LDOC; after LDOC, no late assignments will be accepted, regardless of how long it's been since the original due date. This is necessary to ensure we have enough time to grade all your work prior to our final exam date.

If you earn less than a 7 on a regular assignment--that is, a 6.9 or lower--you have the option to redo and resubmit it for regrading--if you can get it in within the late submission window described above (two weeks after the due date, up until LDOC). The resubmission will be treated as a late assignment and will incur late penalties on the schedule described above (-0.143 per day late), but if your original submission was very poor, it might be worth it.

The final project is due on our final exam date. Therefore, no late final projects will be accepted.

Requests for reasonable extensions will be considered when requested in advance—preferably 24, but at least 12, hours before the assignment is due.

Please see the previous section for quiz and exam rescheduling and make-up policies.

Honor code

Every time you turn in an assignment at this University, you implicitly agree—whether or not you sign anything—that you have neither given nor received unauthorized aid, that the work you present is your own, and that you’ve properly cited and attributed all source materials.

Plagiarism is an especially serious breach of UNC’s Honor Code and of your own intellectual and personal integrity. It includes any use of another’s words, ideas, or research—whether quoted, paraphrased, or mentioned—without proper and full citation and attribution. This applies to materials taken from any source, online or off, including code.

Note that assignment/exam submissions containing code fully or primarily authored by someone else will also be considered plagiarism, even if that code was offered freely by its author for use and re-use, and even if you cite your source. The code you write in this class must be primarily your own original work. A more detailed description of how much and what kind of code you’re permitted to borrow and use in your assignments in this class (and why) is available here.

Please also note that although collaboration with other COMP 126 students may be permitted on certain assignments, it is absolutely prohibited on any aspect of any quiz or exam.

If you do not understand the definitions of plagiarism, citation, attribution, or “unauthorized aid”, it is your responsibility to remedy that; ignorance will not be accepted as an excuse. UNC Libraries' plagiarism tutorial is a good place to start: https://guides.lib.unc.edu/plagiarism/purpose

Please feel free to contact me with any questions or for additional clarification.

Because it is neither my responsibility nor my place to determine whether breaches of the Honor Code are intentional or unintentional, I will immediately report all suspected violations to the Honor Court, which may result in a failing grade, probation, suspension, or expulsion.
